**Investment Game Concept Note**

**GEF Project:**

The GEF-funded project “Scaling Financial and Information Services for
Smallholder Adaptation Project in Zambia and Uganda”, led by the
Alliance of Bioversity and CIAT, seeks to understand how to **scale
various bundles of financial and information services** to smallholders.
A core component of the impact evaluation of this service provision is
providing different bundles of products to randomly chosen sets of
farmers.

**Motivation: Bundling of financial services**

While bundling of services is a core practice for the partner
organizations in this project, little is known about **how bundling of
services affects demand for and attention to individual bundle
components**. Critically, if bundling of one good or service with
another makes smallholders less knowledgeable about individual
components while simultaneously raising the price of either good/service
for consumers uninterested in one of the components, this could reduce
the demand for welfare-enhancing goods and services. Critically this is
a question that will not be able to be answered as part of the other
impact assessment activities, as it requires **randomizing whether the
same goods are offered to consumers both bundled and unbundled**. Hence,
we conduct a **lab-in-the-field experiment** that will answer the
following questions:

**Primary Research questions:**

1.  How does offering seeds and insurance as a bundle vs separate
    products affect demand for insurance?

2.  How does offering seeds and insurance as a bundle vs separate
    products affect information seeking behavior around how insurance
    works while making a purchase decision?

3.  How does offering seeds and insurance as a bundle vs. separate
    products affect investments in other risky technologies
    (fertilizer)?

**Secondary Research questions:**

4.  How does experience with a virtual learning game affect actual
    demand for bundles offered in the real world?

5.  How does demand for bundled products differ across demographic
    groups (e.g., gender, age)?

**Logistics:**

The experiment will be administered via a **tablet application** to be
**co-designed** with a programmer from **ABC**. Administration will be
done by project partners: One Acre Fund and Solidaridad, during visits
to participants in which they offer goods and services as part of the
main impact evaluations. The tablet experiment should be conducted
*before* goods and services are offered, in order to answer research
question \#4. Most instructors will (ideally) be programmed as audio
content within the app itself, in order to minimize burden on field
staff. If this is not feasible, we will provide instructional scripts
for enumerators to read to participants. Ideally, enumerators will be
able to hand the respondent the tablet and mostly just be present to
answer questions as needed.

The tablet experiment will be administered to 800 farmers/partner in
Uganda and 800 farmers/partner in Zambia. (This means 1600/ country if
both partners are present). The farmers must be randomly selected in
equal proportions from the control group, group B1, group B2, and group
B3 of the main impact evaluation. Specifically, this means:

| Participant Numbers | Partner 1/ Country 1 | Partner 1/ Country 2 | Partner 2/ Country 1 | Partner 2/ Country 2 |
|----|----|----|----|----|
| Control | 200 | 200 | 200 | 200 |
| B1 | 200 | 200 | 200 | 200 |
| B2 | 200 | 200 | 200 | 200 |
| B3 | 200 | 200 | 200 | 200 |

Partners will provide a list of individuals in each experimental group
and in each country, and IFPRI will conduct the randomization to
determine the selected set of participations

***Materials required from partners to implement the experiment**
include* **tablets** (to administer the experimental application and
administer a short survey) and **funds** of on average \$3/participant
to administer **incentive** payments from the game. (Actual payouts will
vary slightly depending on how participants complete the exercise.)

**Experiment description:**

The experiment asks individuals to make simulated agricultural input
purchase decisions. These decisions happen in 3 rounds meant to
represent agricultural seasons, including a practice round meant to
allow respondents to familiarize themselves with the tablet application
and task. The latter two rounds will be used for analysis.

Each round begins by endowing participants with **25 “tokens”** to use
for investments during that round only; tokens do not transfer between
rounds. The use of generic tokens allows for differential conversion
with local currencies in the two study countries. Participants must
allocate their budget between safe "savings" (tokens kept in the
lockbox) and risky agricultural "inputs.” Risk arises through the
channel of **weather**. For simplicity, there are two possible weather
realizations: good rains and poor rains (drought). In the game, good
rains occur with an 80% chance, and poor rains happen with a 20% chance.
Agricultural investment in the context of the game is an all-or-nothing
prospect: in good rain years, investments yield high returns, but in bad
rain years, the crop fails entirely, and all tokens spent on inputs are
lost, leaving the participant with only the tokens they chose not to
invest. Of course, such features are not necessarily fully realistic.
However, **the goal of the experiment is not to mimic reality entirely**
(otherwise we wouldn’t need a lab experiment at all), but to **construct
a decision environment that mimics some features of reality**, is
**simple** enough for participants to easily understand, and allows us
to **answer the research questions** at hand.

The experiment begins with an **instructional phase and one
non-incentivized practice** **round** to ensure participants understand
the parameters of the game. Then participants play **two incentivized
rounds**. There are **two versions of the second round**, which will be
**randomized** between participants (50% will receive Version A and 50%
will receive version B.)

1.  **Round 1:** Participants are given the option of purchasing up to
    **10 tokens** worth of **fertilizer**. (They must save at least 15
    tokens in this round). Investment in fertilizer is risky; for each
    token invested, they will receive **2 tokens** in return if there
    are **good rains**, and **0 tokens** if there are **bad rains**.
    (See table below for summary of input prices and payouts.) Any
    tokens not spent are saved. Savings has both no risk and no
    reward—the number of tokens saved is the number of tokens earned.
    After participants make their fertilizer investment choice, they see
    whether they ended up getting good or bad weather (which is random
    with 80/20 chances as explained above). They then see a short
    summary screen showing how much they spent on inputs vs. saved, and
    how much they earned at the end of the round.

2.  **Round 2—Version A (Unbundled inputs):** Participants are still
    given the option of purchasing up to 10 tokens worth of fertilizer.
    However, they can now also purchase **improved seeds** if they’d
    like (**cost of 10 tokens**) and **insurance** for the cost of those
    seeds (**2 tokens**). If there are good rains, the **seeds pay out
    30 tokens**, and if there are **bad rains** they pay out **0
    tokens**. The **insurance** pays out **nothing** under **good
    rains** but **10 tokens** under **bad rains** (representing the
    price of the seeds). \[Hence note, that this is the actuarily fair
    price of insurance in this case.\] After participants are informed
    of this possibility and **given limited information about the
    insurance** (they are only informed of the **price** and that the
    insurance might help them if there are bad rains, with no specific
    details of how), they are given the option to **pay 1 token** from
    their budget for the round to **first watch a video explaining the
    insurance** or to proceed directly to the investment task, which
    proceeds similarly to round 1 (with but with additional input
    purchase options).

3.  **Round 2—Version B (Bundled inputs):** Similarly to version A,
    participants can buy up to 10 tokens worth of fertilizer. However,
    they can now also purchase a **bundle of improved seeds and
    insurance** if they’d like. The bundled product is just the
    **combination of the two products** offered in Version A, so it
    **costs 12 tokens**, pays out **30 tokens** under **good rains**,
    and pays out **10 tokens** under **bad rains** (representing the
    price of the seeds). After participants are informed of this
    possibility and **given limited information about the insurance**
    (they are only informed of the bundle **price** and that the
    included insurance might help them if there are bad rains, with no
    specific details of how), they are given the option to **pay 1
    token** from their budget for the round to **first watch a video
    explaining the insurance** or to proceed directly to the investment
    task, which proceeds similarly to round 1 (with but with additional
    input purchase options).

After both rounds are played, the **number of tokens earned in rounds 1
and 2** is **totaled** and **converted to local currency**.

Finally, respondents will take a **short survey** administered by the
enumerators asking about their basic demographic information, and a few
comprehension questions about the game. Once the survey is completed,
respondents will then **be paid their earnings** from the game in local
currency.

Input Payout Table:

| Input | Rounds | Max. Units | Price/unit | Good State Payoff/unit (80%) | Bad State Payoff/unit (20%) |
|----|----|----|----|----|----|
| Fertilizer | Practice, 1, 2 | 10 | 1 | 2 | 0 |
| Improved seeds | 2-Version A | 1 | 10 | 30 | 0 |
| Insurance | 2-Version A | 1 | 2 | 0 | 10 |
| Improved Seeds + Insurance Bundle | 2-Version B | 1 | 12 | 30 | 10 |

**Connection between design and research questions:**

1.  **Demand and Information Seeking (Questions 1 and 2)**

To measure the **impact of bundling on demand** and **cognitive
engagement**, the experiment varies the **transparency** of the
insurance premium. In Version A (unbundled inputs), insurance is
presented as an optional add-on (2 tokens) to the seed cost (10 tokens),
requiring an active choice to "opt-in". In Version B (Bundled), the
products are integrated into a single 12-token price point. To address
information-seeking behavior, both versions include a choice for
participants to watch an explanatory video for a cost of 1 token. The
system tracks whether participants choose to learn how the insurance
mechanism functions (recouping the 10-token seed cost during bad rains)
before finalizing their investment. We can also use the survey questions
on **comprehension** to see whether bundling affects not just
information seeking, but also understanding of the products in the game.

2.  **Effects on Input Investment (Question 3)**

The design further explores how the offer of bundled vs. unbundled
products affects broader risk-taking, by introducing fertilizer as an
**additional risky input**. Since fertilizer yields are tied to weather
outcomes (doubling in good rain but resulting in total loss during
drought), having a seed-insurance safety net may encourage (crowd in)
additional investments in other risky technologies. Critically, though
the provision of insurance itself is not randomized (and hence we can’t
look at the effect of insurance provision on investment directly), the
offer of a bundled/unbundled product is. Given that the offer type
itself will likely affect demand and attention seeking, these are the
channels that would likely explain the effects (and the offer type can
be used as an instrument for demand/attention).

3.  **Experience with Learning (Question 4)**

There is some evidence that learning in virtual environments can
translate into differences in real world decision-making. Allowing some
farmers to complete this activity and learn about insurance may **affect
their take-up of similar products** offered in the **real world**. To
see if this is the case, we can compare the take-up of products offered
to farmers as part of this project between those who also participate in
this lab experiment and those that don’t. **Note:** this is also why it
is important to make sure experimental participants are equally split
between the impact assessment condition groups (control, B1, B2, and
B3); we don’t want participation in this game to systematically bias the
demand of one group and not others.

4.  **Heterogeneity in demand (Question 5)**

Along with this experimental game, we will also ask participants to
provide some simple demographic information (gender, age, education
level, geographical area, etc.). This will allow us to compare behavior
in the game across demographic groups. Information about differences in
demand can then be used by partners to better target product offers or
product sensitization strategies.
